Shared Community Resolve

A community's shared understanding of a problem and how to address it is essential for effective action, including collective ownership and united commitment to follow through.

What Is Shared Community Resolve?

A community's shared understanding of a problem and how to address it is essential for effective action, including a common recognition of the issue and its causes, a shared vision of possible solutions, a collective sense of ownership over decisions, and a united commitment to follow through.

While outside ideas can inspire action, it is ultimately the community's collective experience, decision-making, manpower, and financial resources that drive meaningful progress.

How to Recognize It

You may already have community resolve. This is how to recognize if you do, so that you can amplify what exists, and either build or work around what does not exist.

  • Do you have a formal or informal body that makes shared decisions to address your crisis?
  • Does your decision makers include stakeholders from all affected communities?
  • Do all decision makers need to come to agreement about actions?
  • Have your decision makers mapped histories of the development of your crisis?
  • Have your decision makers agreed on what event triggered the vicious cycle of decline?
  • Have your decision makers made a list of all elements of your vicious cycle that are changeable through changed actions?
  • Have your decision makers determined one key change that, if successfully carried out, may be powerful enough to reverse the cycle of decline?
  • Do all decision makers need to participate in carrying out the agreement?
  • Do all decision makers need to provide time, labor or resources to carry out the agreement?
  • Do your agreements include time markers for carrying out actions and measuring success?
  • Has your community developed protocols to support those members taking the greatest risks to their livelihood in order to carry out the decision?

If You Have It: Amplify It

You may already have community resolve. This is how to recognize if you do, so that you can amplify what exists, and either build or work around what does not exist.

  • Does your decision making body have protocol for reevaluating decisions? Why it is important and strategies used to be inclusive of dissent.
  • How to enable those with dissenting opinions to support agreements? Why it is important and strategies used to be inclusive of dissent.

If You Don't, Try to Build It

Shared Community Resolve is critical for successful change, and if you do not have, there are strategies for building elements of it.

  • How to enable those with dissenting opinions to support agreements? Why it is important and strategies used to be inclusive of dissent.
  • How to map a history of the development of your crisis? Why it is important and strategies used to map that history.
  • How to include all stakeholders in providing man power or financial resources. Why it is important and strategies used to give everyone a functional role.

Otherwise: Work Around It

Since Shared Community Resolve is critical for successful change, if it is not possible, then mitigation is necessary. Here are strategies for mitigating lack of Community Resolve.

See It In Action

Apo Island, Philippines: Marine Sanctuary

Apo Islanders reversed the collapse of their coral reef and fisheries by creating a marine sanctuary on 10% of their fishing grounds and establishing community management across the entire area.

Drawing on traditional decision-making practices, representatives from all island families assembled to discuss the crisis until reaching consensus, even if it took days.

Sukhomajri, India: Watershed Restoration

Villagers transformed barren hillsides into lush forests by creating a shared system of water harvesting that gave everyone a stake in protecting the watershed.

The community agreed that water rights would be distributed equally, motivating collective action to protect the land that captured their water supply.

Gaviotas, Colombia: Sustainable Community

A community built in the barren savannas of Colombia developed innovative technologies and reforested thousands of hectares through shared vision and commitment.

The community's resolve to create a self-sustaining settlement drove decades of experimentation and ecological restoration.
