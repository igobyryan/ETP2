Shared Community Resolve

A community's shared understanding of a problem and how to address it is essential for effective action.

Related Ingredients

→ Outside stimulation and facilitation → Enduring commitment of local leadership → Overcoming social obstacles

A community's shared understanding of a problem and how to address it is essential for effective action, including a common recognition of the issue and its causes, a shared vision of possible solutions, a collective sense of ownership over decisions, and a united commitment to follow through. While outside ideas can inspire action, it is ultimately the community's collective experience, decision-making, manpower, and financial resources that drive meaningful progress.

Recognize It

The checklist below will help you to recognize what facets of community resolve you already have, so that you can amplify what exists, and either build or work around what does not exist.

  • Do you have a formal or informal body for addressing your crisis that involves stakeholders from various parts of the community?

    Having community members with a range of skills and experience at the decision making table takes advantage of the whole community's knowledge. It also broadens support for actions that require substantial commitment and sacrifice, and provides the financial and labor resources to carry out the actions.

  • Do your decision makers have a process for choosing actions that all can agree upon and participate in carrying it out?

    A real sense of collective ownership of decisions is needed for the risks involved in taking action, and the commitment of time, labor or financial resources needed to carry it out. A strong decision making body provides ways for all members to contribute, commits to finding common ground, and supports those members taking the greatest risks to their livelihood, so that they can participate.

  • Do your decision makers have a shared understanding of what the problem is and how it occurred?

    Choosing a course of action requires a shared vision of the nature and source of the crisis, so that actions are directed at core problems. Gathering the knowledge, memories, and experience of the community allows decision makers to map the events that triggered the vicious cycle of decline.

  • Has your group determined key actions that, if successfully carried out, may be powerful enough to reverse the cycle of decline?

    Many changes are possible, but not all are powerful enough to set change in motion. By mapping which elements of your vicious cycle are changeable, and brainstorming what can be done to change that element, it becomes easier to see which actions are both doable and potentially impactful enough. Any action that does not have enough force is just swimming against the current.

  • Is the community committed to the action?

    Although ideas stimulating action may come from outside, the community needs to move forward with its own decisions, manpower, and financial resources.

Amplify It

If you have elements of Shared Community Resolve in place, it is important to nurture and amplify them, since they are pivotal to success.

Increase the diversity of your stakeholders

Having stakeholders from various parts of the community will increase chances of success. It brings a breadth of knowledge, memory, and experience to the table to help understand shared problems and imagine solutions. It also broadens support for decisions that require substantial commitment and sacrifice and provides the financial and labor resources to carry out the actions.

Have all decision makers contributing manpower, financial support or other resources

Direct participation in carrying out decisions strengthens engagement and resolve while providing the funds and labor needed to succeed. This requires planning inclusive strategies for contribution, so that everyone has a functional role that matches their abilities or resources. Build in flexibility

It is natural for a course of action to need modification based on unfolding results, increased awareness of the resources needed for that action, or new social obstacles that emerge. It is important to have protocols for communities to improve choices based on experience. Agreed upon benchmarks and procedures for reevaluating and modifying shared decisions builds flexibility into action plans so that they have greater chances of success.

Build It

Shared Community Resolve is critical for successful change, and if you do not have, there are strategies for building elements of it.

Interview community members who have special knowledge about the crisis

There are a wide range of community members who have specialized knowledge about your crisis, including scientists, educators, elders, those working or managing the land, those involved in managing household resources. By reaching out to them to pool their experience with the crisis, you create a strong foundation for mapping problems and solutions. It also builds a base of community members who are now involved with your work and helps identify individuals who may have the time, commitment, and disposition for joining forces to make decisions.

Assemble a group to map your vicious cycle of decline

An ETP flowchart uses knowledge pooled from your community to identify what triggered decline, what elements in the vicious cycle are changeable, and which of those may be a powerful enough lever to reverse the vicious cycle. By doing the mapping process as a group, you can arrive at a shared understanding of the problem and potential solutions.

Work Around It

Shared Community Resolve is essential for turning around an ecological crisis endangering a community. Reversing ecological decline can only happen if many people risk changing how they manage the land that supports them. Since committing to changing their behavior may place real burdens on people and even threaten their immediate livelihood and security, enough people need to feel commitment because they share a vision of what has gone wrong and how to fix it.

Challenges to working together are situation-dependent, but there are some common obstacles. Here are strategies for mitigating lack of Community Resolve:

Focus efforts to collaborate on the action that is powerful enough to turn things around

Mapping vicious cycles and levers

Mapping your vicious cycles with the ETP flowcharts helps to identify what actions may have enough force.

Set differences aside

Ideology, morals, and even reasons for wanting change are common obstacles to collaborating on addressing shared problems. If differences can be set aside to address the specific actions that may restore the ecosystem, common ground can be found without having to see eye-to-eye on other crucial matters.

Coalition building strategies
  • ICA post-its
  • Coalition building

Address overburdened community members

Another common obstacle is community members being overburdened with demands on their time in order to survive.

Support strategies
  • Support systems for those affected most
  • Commitment to building upon any success with programs supporting other social needs (spin-offs and social and ecological diversity)

Develop benchmarks to measure and share success

Every success can shore up commitment and make more people willing to participate.

See It In Action

Apo Island, Philippines: Marine Sanctuary

Apo Islanders reversed the collapse of their coral reef and fisheries by creating a marine sanctuary on 10% of their fishing grounds and establishing community management across the entire area.

Drawing on traditional decision-making practices, representatives from all island families assembled to discuss the crisis until reaching consensus, even if it took days.

Sukhomajri, India: Watershed Restoration

Villagers transformed barren hillsides into lush forests by creating a shared system of water harvesting that gave everyone a stake in protecting the watershed.

The community agreed that water rights would be distributed equally, motivating collective action to protect the land that captured their water supply.

Gaviotas, Colombia: Sustainable Community

A community built in the barren savannas of Colombia developed innovative technologies and reforested thousands of hectares through shared vision and commitment.

The community's resolve to create a self-sustaining settlement drove decades of experimentation and ecological restoration.
