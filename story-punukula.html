Success Story

Punukula, India

Escaping the Pesticide Trap

The suicide notes from Andhra Pradesh farmers often mentioned the same thing: debt. By the late 1990s, the state had the highest farmer suicide rate in India. The method of choice was an insecticide cocktail — the same chemicals that had trapped them in debt in the first place.

In the village of Punukula, a community of about 900 people, one family's crisis became the turning point. When a prominent elder's son collapsed from acute pesticide poisoning and spent a week unconscious, the hospital bill pushed the family's debt to a breaking point. The elder, Margam Mutthaiah, decided he had nothing left to lose by trying something different.

Within two years, every farmer in Punukula had stopped using chemical pesticides. Within a decade, 340,000 farmers across 3,170 villages had followed the same path.

The Trap

Cotton arrived in Andhra Pradesh in the early 1980s with the promise of cash income. Small-scale farmers who had grown millet, sorghum, peanuts, and vegetables for home consumption and local sale saw an opportunity. Growing cotton required pesticides and chemical fertilizers — inputs these mostly illiterate farmers had never used before. For guidance, they relied on local agrochemical dealers who sold seeds, chemicals, and fertilizers on credit and offered advice supplied by the multinational companies behind the products.

The early years worked. Yields were strong, debt was manageable, money was new. Then the pests arrived — bollworms, leafworms, aphids — and the chemistry that had seemed so powerful started losing the battle. Repeated spraying killed the most susceptible insects and left the resistant ones to reproduce. As resistance spread through pest populations, farmers sprayed more often and mixed more chemicals, sometimes combining ten different insecticides in a single application. The spraying killed the birds, wasps, beetles, and spiders that had previously controlled pests naturally. With those predators gone, cutting back on chemicals meant losing the crop entirely.

The trap closed in every direction. Pesticide costs escalated as chemical fertilizers became necessary to compensate for soil stripped of natural fertility. The cost of producing cotton exceeded what cotton sold for. Debt compounded. Dealers who held the debt warned farmers: stop buying chemicals and repay everything immediately. Children went into indentured labor to service family debts. Education stopped. Everyone on the farm — including children — sprayed toxic chemicals by hand, and chronic headaches, nausea, skin problems, and neurological damage became ordinary facts of life.

One Farmer, One Season

Around 1998, a nonprofit worker named Karnati Venu Madhav began talking to Punukula farmers about Non-Pesticide Management — NPM, a pest control approach developed by the Center for Sustainable Agriculture using neem and other natural methods. Madhav had grown up in a farming family with the same problems. His organization, SECURE, arranged for some villagers to travel 400 kilometers to see a farm where NPM was actually working. They saw it with their own eyes. They remained skeptical.

Then Mutthaiah's son nearly died. The hospital bill was the last straw. Mutthaiah, a respected village elder with deep agricultural debt, agreed to try NPM on his fields. SECURE coached him through it.

Neem — a common tree throughout India — was the foundation. Its natural compounds interfere with insect feeding, growth, and egg production without harming birds, predatory insects, or humans. Seeds are ground to powder, soaked in water, and sprayed every ten days. Neem cake applied to soil doubles as organic fertilizer. The only cost is labor. Complementing the neem: chili-garlic spray for severe infestations, cow dung and urine mixture to deter sucking insects, a naturally occurring virus that kills bollworms, trap crops planted at field margins to draw pests away from cotton, sticky boards for whiteflies, bonfires on moonless nights to attract moths, bird perches to enlist natural predators. Deep summer plowing to disrupt pest life cycles in the soil. Vermicompost to rebuild soil fertility without chemicals.

Mutthaiah's harvest was as good as his neighbors' who were still spraying — and he hadn't spent money on insecticides. Twenty farmers tried NPM the following season. SECURE stationed two staff members in the village, a man and a woman, to teach and assist. Women pushed their husbands to stop using the chemicals that were making their families sick. The results held. The year after that, more farmers converted.

By 2000, every farmer in Punukula used NPM on cotton. In 2004, the village council formally declared Punukula pesticide-free and extended vermicomposting across the village, eliminating the need for chemical fertilizers entirely.

What Came Back

Pest predator populations recovered once the chemicals stopped. As birds, wasps, and beetles returned, even neem applications became less necessary — nature reasserted its own control. Medical expenses dropped. Debt became payable. When dealers tried to demand immediate full repayment, the village negotiated collectively and won.

Freed indentured children returned to school through special catch-up courses. Women who had gathered and processed neem for their own farms began selling neem and other NPM materials to villages across the region — a new income source and a new source of standing. The village set up a marketing cooperative when dealers tried to underpay for NPM cotton. They lobbied government. They built a small cotton ginning operation. They purified their drinking water. The capacity to fight pesticide companies — and win — turned out to be transferable to every other problem they faced.

The suicide epidemic ended.

Punukula became a destination. Thousands of farmers from other villages came to see what had happened. Multinational pesticide corporations lobbied the state government to suppress NPM. The government did the opposite, incorporating NPM into its statewide rural development program and disseminating it through women's self-help groups. By 2008, 340,000 farmers across 3,170 villages were using NPM on nearly a million acres — not just cotton, but grain and vegetable crops too.

How the System Changed

Recognizing it began with the visit to a working NPM farm 400 kilometers away. Seeing wasn't enough to overcome skepticism, but it established that escape was possible. What actually broke through was a personal crisis — Mutthaiah's son's poisoning made the cost of staying in the system undeniable in a way that abstract argument hadn't. SECURE's role was to be present, patient, and ready when a community member was ready to try.

Mapping it had already been done, in a sense, by the Center for Sustainable Agriculture. The feedback loops were well understood: pesticide use killed natural predators, which increased pest pressure, which required more pesticides, which increased resistance, which required even more pesticides. The map pointed clearly to the lever: restore natural predator populations by eliminating chemicals, and the system would begin to correct itself.

Reversing it required solving a collective action problem first. Individual farmers couldn't switch to NPM safely while neighbors continued spraying — chemicals drift, pests migrate, and a single unprotected field is vulnerable. SECURE's presence in the village enabled the coordinated conversion that made individual risk manageable. When enough farmers switched together, the critical mass of natural predator recovery made the whole approach more viable for everyone.

Locking it in happened on multiple fronts simultaneously. The village council's formal pesticide-free declaration in 2004 made the change institutional. The women's neem supply network created economic stakes in NPM's continuation. The marketing cooperative meant farmers no longer depended on the dealers who had trapped them. Each new enterprise — cotton ginning, water purification, the cooperative — added another layer of independence from the systems that had made them vulnerable.

The spread beyond Punukula came through visibility and government alignment. Visitors came, saw, and carried the model home. When the state government chose to promote rather than suppress NPM, the infrastructure of women's self-help groups provided a distribution network that no nonprofit could have built. Local success became state policy, which became one of the largest pesticide reduction programs in the world.

Key Ingredients

Outside stimulation and facilitation was the ignition, not the engine. SECURE provided technical training, arranged the critical site visit, and kept staff present in the village long enough for adoption to reach critical mass. But the organization's most important act was helping farmers understand a system they were already living inside — giving them the conceptual tools to see the trap as a trap.

Letting nature do the work was literal here. NPM succeeded because it restored the ecological relationships that chemical farming had destroyed. Once predator populations recovered, the system's own dynamics shifted in favor of the farmers. Neem worked because it had evolved over millions of years to defeat plant-eating insects — human ingenuity borrowed what nature had already designed.

Social and ecological memory made the transition thinkable. NPM wasn't a foreign technology — it was a modern, systematic version of traditional farming that preceded chemical agriculture. Neem had been used in India for centuries. The knowledge of how to farm without chemicals hadn't vanished entirely; it needed to be recovered and organized.

Overcoming social obstacles required collective action that individual farmers couldn't achieve alone. The dealer debt system was designed to make exit impossible. SECURE's sustained presence made coordinated conversion feasible. The village's united front against dealer pressure — negotiating collectively rather than each family facing intimidation alone — was what made debt resolution possible.

Shared community awareness and commitment was built as much by women as by male farmers. Women had been spraying chemicals alongside their husbands and children. They understood what the chemicals were doing to their families' health. Their pressure on reluctant husbands to adopt NPM, and their subsequent role in producing and distributing NPM materials across the region, turned a farming technique into a community transformation.

Building resilience compounded with each new initiative. Debt clearance improved financial resilience. Diverse income streams — neem sales, the cooperative, cotton ginning, tourism from visiting farmers — reduced vulnerability to any single point of failure. The political confidence that came from confronting and defeating multinational corporations made the community harder to exploit in the future. What started as a farming technique became the foundation of a community that knew how to organize, negotiate, and win.

Research and documentation: Dr. Gerald G. Marten, EcoTipping Points Project

Community partner: Punukula Village, Andhra Pradesh, India; Center for Sustainable Agriculture, Hyderabad
