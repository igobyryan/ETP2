Tool Overview

Understanding Systemic Change

When communities face environmental challenges like depleted fisheries, degraded forests, or water shortages, the problems are rarely simple. They're usually the result of interconnected causes that reinforce each other—what we call vicious cycles. A vicious cycle is when one problem makes another problem worse, which in turn makes the first problem worse, creating a downward spiral.

What This Tool Does

This tool guides you through a structured process to gather community knowledge, map vicious cycles, identify intervention points, evaluate which interventions make sense, and lock in gains by building mutually reinforcing success.

Who This Tool Is For

Community groups, NGO practitioners, and local leaders facing interconnected environmental and social challenges.

Time Required

Multiple sessions over 2-4 weeks, depending on community size and complexity.

Part 1: Recognize It

Identifying Vicious Cycles

Diagnose the Problem: Identify the circular patterns driving decline and the leverage points where intervention can reverse them.

As you prepare to map your situation, these are the key patterns to look for. Understanding these dynamics will help you see how problems connect and reinforce each other, leading to crisis.

What to Look For:

Interconnected Problems

Are you facing multiple problems that seem related rather than isolated issues? For example, not just "fewer fish" but also "damaged coral reefs," "fishers increasingly using destructive methods," and "declining income."

Progressive Worsening Over Time

Have problems gotten worse year by year, or even accelerated? This suggests reinforcing cycles at work rather than random fluctuations.

A Shift from a Better Past

Can community elders remember when things were different—when fish were more plentiful, forests were healthier, water was more accessible? Understanding what changed is crucial.

The "Trigger" or Initial Change

Was there a specific action or event that started the decline? This could be a new technology introduced or an old one abandoned, a change in land management or ownership, policy changes, population growth, or other pressures.

Circular Patterns of Cause and Effect

Do you see situations where Problem A worsens Problem B, which worsens Problem C, which then worsens Problem A again? These feedback loops are the hallmark of vicious cycles, and are what must be reversed.

Thresholds or Tipping Points

Have there been moments when the situation suddenly got much worse? Or are you worried about crossing a point of no return?

[Visual Diagram: Simple Vicious Cycle — For Placement Only]

Example: Apo Island, Philippines

Community members could see interconnected problems: declining fish catches, damaged coral reefs from dynamite and cyanide fishing, and increasing poverty. Elders remembered when the reefs were healthy and fish were abundant.

The trigger was the introduction of destructive fishing methods. This created a vicious cycle: destructive fishing methods → reef damage → fewer fish → more destructive fishing methods → more reef damage.

A threshold was reached when fishermen could no longer make a living from the island fishing grounds, and had to move further from home to find fish. This only created new vicious cycles amplifying the first, because fishing further away → less incentive to protect fishery → more destructive fishing methods → more reef damage → fewer fish.

Eventually, there was nowhere close by with adequate fish, and their community faced collapse.

Part 2: Map It

Understanding Your Vicious Cycles

Create a diagram showing the feedback loops that have been driving decline in your community.

This section provides step-by-step guidance for bringing the community together to understand what's causing their problems. You'll create a boxes-and-arrows diagram showing the feedback loops that have been driving decline.

01

Convene Your Mapping Group

Who to Include:

  • Elders who remember how the situation developed over time
  • Women who often hold passed-down knowledge and may observe different aspects of community life
  • Young people who understand current realities and possibilities
  • People from different roles in the community (farmers, fishers, merchants, etc.)
  • If you're an outside NGO, include diverse community members in this process
  • People with a history of community service

How to Convene:

  • Meet in a neutral, accessible space where all participants feel comfortable
  • Plan for 2-3 hours for initial mapping session
  • Ensure language and format work for all participants
  • Consider whether mixed-gender or separate groups work better in your context

Ingredient: Building Shared Vision

This process of gathering diverse community members to understand your situation together isn't just about analysis—it's building shared vision and commitment. When people participate in mapping the problem, they develop a common understanding and ownership of solutions.

02

Start With the Story

First Question

Think back to the very first action or activity that began to set the decline in motion. This is what we call the "negative tipping point lever"—the trigger that started things going in the wrong direction.

Additional Guiding Questions:

  • What was different before this change happened?
  • When did you first notice things getting worse?
  • What else changed around that time?

Process:

  • Have participants share stories and memories
  • One person should capture key points (use large paper visible to all, or Post-it notes)
  • Look for: triggering events, changes in behavior, environmental shifts, outside pressures
  • Don't worry about getting everything perfect—you'll refine as you go

Example: Apo Island

On Apo Island in the Philippines, elders remembered when fish were plentiful and coral reefs were healthy. The negative tipping point lever was the combination of population growth and the introduction of destructive fishing methods like dynamite and cyanide fishing in the 1970s and 1980s.

03

Map the First Feedback Loop

Key Question

What happened in the ecosystem or community to form a circular chain of effects—a feedback loop—that kept the decline going?

Process:

  1. Start with the trigger/negative tipping point lever you identified
  2. Ask: What did this cause or make worse?
  3. Then: What did that cause or make worse?
  4. Continue until you complete a circle back to something that reinforces the original problem
  5. Draw this as boxes (the problems/factors) connected by arrows (showing what affects what)

The boxes in your diagram represent:

  • The tipping point lever (the trigger)
  • The "bottom line" (the main problem you're trying to solve, like declining fish stocks or degraded forest)
  • Any ecosystem or community elements that complete the feedback loop

Arrows between boxes show what affects what.

[Visual Diagram: Apo Island First Feedback Loop — For Placement Only]

Example: Apo Island — First Feedback Loop

Destructive fishing methods (negative tipping point lever) →
Coral reefs damaged →
Fish breeding grounds destroyed →
Declining fish stocks (bottom line) →
Fishers use even more destructive methods to catch remaining fish

[cycle repeats and intensifies]

04

Add Reinforcing Feedback Loops

Key Question

What happened after that to intensify the decline even more?

Process: Look for additional circular chains of effects that reinforced the first feedback loop. Add more boxes and arrows to your diagram to show how the decline expanded or accelerated.

You may find:

  • Second-order effects (the decline caused other problems that made things worse)
  • Social dynamics that reinforced environmental decline
  • Economic pressures that developed as a result
  • Multiple reinforcing loops working together
[Visual Diagram: Apo Island Reinforcing Loops — For Placement Only]

Example: Apo Island — Adding Reinforcing Loops

The first loop was reinforced by economic and social dynamics:

Declining catches → Reduced income → Economic desperation → More intensive use of destructive methods → Further reef damage

And another loop:

Fishers traveling farther → Less time protecting local waters → Reduced community cohesion → Weaker informal rules → More destructive fishing

Part 3: Reverse It

Breaking the Cycle

Identify leverage points and design interventions that can reverse vicious cycles into virtuous ones.

Now that you understand the vicious cycles driving decline, it's time to identify where you can intervene to reverse them. The goal is to find actions that can break the negative feedback loops and start positive ones.

01

Identify Potential Leverage Points

Look at your vicious cycle diagram and ask:

Where could you intervene?

  • Which connections between problems could be broken?
  • Which elements could be replaced with positive alternatives?
  • Where is the community ready and able to act?
  • Are there examples from other communities that succeeded?

What resources or support do you have access to?

  • Community skills and knowledge
  • Natural resources that could regenerate
  • Traditional practices that worked in the past
  • Outside support (NGOs, government programs, etc.)
  • Economic opportunities tied to restoration

Ingredient: Community Resolve

The evaluation process helps surface whether you have the resolve needed. If community support seems low for a crucial intervention, that's valuable information—you may need to build resolve first through education and dialogue.

02

Envision the Positive Flip

For each potential intervention point, imagine what success would look like:

  • If this intervention works, what positive changes would ripple through the system?
  • Could it create new positive feedback loops that reinforce themselves?
  • How would the boxes and arrows in your diagram change?

Example: Apo Island

When they created the marine sanctuary:

Fish stocks recovered → More fish spilled over to fishing areas → Catches improved → Community saw benefits → More support for protection → Stronger enforcement → Better recovery

[virtuous cycle]

[Visual Diagram: Virtuous Cycle — For Placement Only]
03

Evaluate Feasibility and Impact

The positive tipping point lever consists of the actions taken to reverse those elements plus the environmental technology used for those actions and the community organization to make the technologies and actions effective.

For each potential intervention, work through these questions as a group:

Impact Questions:

  • How big a change could this make?
  • Does it address a root cause or just a symptom?
  • Will social and environmental gains go hand in hand?
  • Could it trigger positive reinforcement?
  • Is it powerful enough to reverse the bottom line?

Feasibility Questions:

  • What resources would this require? (money, time, skills, materials)
  • Do we have community support for this?
  • What obstacles might we face? (social, political, economic)
  • Do we have (or can we get) what we need to try this?

Risk Questions:

  • What could go wrong?
  • Are there unintended consequences we should consider?
  • Is this reversible if it doesn't work?
  • Will there be pushback from other entities?
  • Will there be encroachment or takeover from other entities?

Process Tool: Simple Comparison Matrix

  • List potential interventions down the left side
  • Use columns for: Potential Impact (High/Medium/Low), Resource Needs, Community Support, Major Obstacles
  • Fill in together as a group
  • Discuss patterns and tradeoffs
[Downloadable Resource: Evaluation Matrix Template — For Placement Only]
04

Choose Your Starting Point

You don't need perfect information to begin. Based on your evaluation:

  • Which 1-3 interventions seem most promising?
  • Is there a logical sequence? (some things need to happen before others)
  • Can you start with something achievable to build momentum?

Remember:

  • You can try multiple interventions, especially if they reinforce each other
  • Starting with a smaller, achievable win can build the community resolve needed for bigger changes
  • You'll learn by trying—your understanding will improve as you act

Part 4: Lock It In

Building Mutually Reinforcing Success

Sustain and Scale: Design for lasting change that reinforces itself and spreads to other communities.

As ecosystem restoration proceeds, the next step is to lock in the gains and increase the sustainability of restoration by embarking on new enterprises that reinforce ecosystem restoration and integrate the restoration with the social fabric of the community.

Key Concept

Early success can die out if it's not built into the fabric of community life. Sustainability comes from creating multiple mutually reinforcing cycles that support each other.

Guiding Questions:

  • What new opportunities does initial success create?
  • How can benefits be shared broadly to build ongoing support?
  • What new activities or enterprises could build on this foundation?
  • How can this become "just the way things are done" rather than a special project?

Example: Apo Island

Initial success (fish recovery) led to:

  • Marine tourism income → Funds for community → More resources for protection
  • Educational programs → Pride in achievement → Stronger community identity
  • Scientific recognition → Outside support → Enhanced credibility

These multiple reinforcing cycles made the marine sanctuary permanent, not just a temporary project.

Process:

  • Revisit this 6-12 months after initial interventions
  • Map the new positive cycles that are emerging
  • Identify opportunities to strengthen and multiply them
  • Look for ways to institutionalize success (community rules, new traditions, ongoing activities)

Ingredient: Success Breeds Success

This is where the "success breeds success" dynamic becomes crucial. Initial wins create opportunities, resources, and motivation for further positive change.
