Success Story

Khao Din, Thailand

When Farming the Forest Brought the Forest Back

By the late 1980s, farmers in Khao Din had cut down every tree they could find. They had to — debt demanded it. The timber money helped, briefly. Then the soil started washing away, the streams dried up, and the crops kept failing no matter how many chemicals they applied. In a village that had arrived decades earlier to find dense jungle full of fish and wild boar, people were going hungry.

In 1988, a team from Save the Children arrived. They didn't bring aid. They brought a question: how did this happen?

The answer changed everything.

The Downward Spiral

When Ajaan Thanawm's family migrated to Nakhon Sawan province in 1954, they found what felt like abundance — thick forest, fertile soil, streams full of fish, food growing wild near the houses. About fifty families settled there. For years, small-scale farming sustained them.

Then in the 1960s, the Thai government, backed by World Bank development models, pushed rural farmers toward export cash crops. An Agricultural Credit Bank extended loans for hybrid seed, chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and equipment. Farmers switched from diversified traditional agriculture to monocropping. They cut more forest to expand fields. For a while, money flowed.

It didn't last. As thousands of farmers grew the same crops, prices fell. Droughts came. Harvests failed. Unable to repay the Agricultural Bank, farmers fell into the hands of informal lenders charging 10% interest per month. Desperate to service their debts, they cut the last remaining trees. With no forest cover, the hillsides baked. Soil lost its capacity to hold moisture. Streams dried up. Chemical costs climbed as soil fertility collapsed, requiring more fertilizer to produce less food.

Eventually families couldn't grow enough to eat. People left for Bangkok. Communities that had been tightly knit fractured. "Everyone was now worried about their own fields and their own family's problems," Thanawm recalls. "For the first time ever, we began to have psychological and social problems. There was little trust and less cooperation." Juvenile delinquency — previously unheard of — appeared. The village had gone from near-abundance to hunger and social disintegration in a single generation.

The Turning Point

The Save the Children team sent to Khao Din in 1986 did something unusual: instead of delivering outside solutions, they helped villagers understand what had happened to them. Through long, sometimes difficult conversations, farmers came to see the feedback loops driving their decline — how debt led to deforestation led to soil collapse led to more debt. "It was amazing! People were lighting up like light bulbs!" recalls team member Andrew Mittelman. "They kept saying: 'My God, what have we done? We couldn't imagine this place of abundance would become a desert.'"

That recognition — collective, not individual — was the turning point. And it contained its own momentum: if the community had made decisions that caused this, they could make decisions that reversed it.

Rebuilding from the Ground Up

The farmers and Save the Children staff began experimenting with agroforestry — mixing trees and crops on the same land in ways that resembled the structure of a natural forest. It wasn't a foreign concept. Their traditional subsistence farming had incorporated many of the same elements. What was different now was the intentionality.

Thanawm was among the first to try it. He planted fruit trees along the periphery of his farm, dug a narrow fish pond with a vegetable island in the middle, and filled the surrounding plots with a rotating mix of fruits, vegetables, medicinal plants, and herbs. The result was a farm that produced food year-round without chemicals, because the diversity of the system did the work that chemicals had been trying to do: natural pest control, organic fertilizer from leaf fall, moisture retention from dense vegetation, soil protection from erosion.

The demonstration worked visibly enough that neighbors adopted similar approaches. As more farms converted, the aggregate effect on the landscape became apparent — water returned to some streams, soil stabilized, food security improved dramatically.

The community also established a community forest on shared land. They wrote rules governing harvest of forest resources and excluded outsiders from cutting. As the forest recovered, animals long thought locally extinct reappeared. So did a sense of collective ownership — of the land, and of the community's ability to shape its own future.

What Compounded

After Save the Children left, Thanawm kept going. He founded the Association of Agriculture, the Environment and Development, Nakhon Sawan, which grew to include 40 villages and 2,545 families practicing locally-adapted versions of agroforestry across thousands of acres. In the late 1990s, the Thai government's "Sufficiency Economy" program — championed by the King — began supporting this approach nationally, giving the community's model institutional recognition it hadn't sought but could use.

The restored forest also turned out to matter beyond the village. Tropical deforestation accounts for 17% of global carbon dioxide emissions. Khao Din's reforestation sequestered carbon at a meaningful scale, linking a local survival story to the global climate problem — without ever framing it that way.

Today Khao Din has 2,500 residents and migration to Bangkok has reversed. The village doesn't generate large cash incomes, and younger generations feel that tension. But the community has something that's hard to rebuild once lost: agency. "We know now that with some careful thinking and a lot of shared effort, we can solve our problems, and fix what is broken," says Thanawm. "Even though we don't have much money, I'm happy."

How the System Changed

Recognizing it started with the Save the Children team's facilitated conversations in 1986. Farmers traced the history of their decline — not as victims of circumstance, but as people who had made choices that triggered reinforcing loops: debt led to deforestation led to soil collapse led to more debt. That shared diagnosis was itself the turning point. Farmers who understood the feedback loops driving their situation could design their way out. Farmers who didn't would have kept applying more chemicals to failing soil.

Mapping it happened through collective problem-solving. The community worked backward through the vicious cycles — export crop dependence, chemical costs, deforestation, erosion, declining yields, more debt — identifying where the loops could be broken. The answer wasn't a single intervention but an integrated shift: agroforestry to restore soil and food security, community forest management to protect the watershed, and a gradual conversion process that didn't require farmers to risk everything at once.

Reversing it began with demonstration plots. Thanawm and a few other willing farmers tried agroforestry first, on land their neighbors could observe. Results were tangible within a single growing season — more food, no chemicals, visible improvement. That made adoption a choice based on evidence rather than a leap of faith.

Locking it in happened through accumulation. Debt paid off. Streams returned. Families stopped leaving for Bangkok. Thanawm founded a regional association that brought the approach to 40 villages. The Thai government's Sufficiency Economy program eventually gave institutional backing to what the community had already proven. And the restored forest began sequestering carbon at scale — connecting a local survival story to the global climate problem without ever being designed to do so.

Key Ingredients

All ten ingredients for success appear in this story, but a few are worth highlighting given how prominently they shaped the outcome.

Outside stimulation and facilitation was essential — but time-limited by design. Save the Children's most important contribution wasn't technical knowledge; it was facilitating collective diagnosis. They left. The community continued.

Shared community awareness and commitment preceded everything else. The "lighting up like light bulbs" moment Mittelman describes wasn't inspiration — it was recognition. Farmers who understood what had happened to their land could own the solution in a way that outside-designed interventions never produce.

Letting nature do the work was the core mechanism of agroforestry. The system succeeded because it mimicked forest structure, allowing ecological processes to restore what chemicals had been trying to replace: soil fertility, moisture retention, pest balance. The community stopped fighting natural systems and put them to work.

Social and ecological memory gave the approach its foundation. Agroforestry wasn't foreign — it resembled the traditional farming these families had practiced before the export crop push. Older villagers remembered what the landscape had looked like. That memory made the new approach feel like recovery rather than experiment.

Enduring commitment of local leadership sustained it after the outside catalyst left. Thanawm kept organizing when there was no institutional support, no funding, and no guarantee the regional network would hold. He did it anyway, and it grew.

Research and documentation: Dr. Gerald G. Marten, Amanda Suutari, EcoTipping Points Project

Community partner: Khao Din Village, Nakhon Sawan Province, Thailand
