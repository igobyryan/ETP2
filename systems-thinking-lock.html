Lock It In

Sustain and Scale: Design for lasting change that reinforces itself and spreads to other communities.

Building Mutually Reinforcing Success

As ecosystem restoration proceeds, the next step is to lock in the gains and increase the sustainability of restoration by embarking on new enterprises that reinforce ecosystem restoration and integrate the restoration with the social fabric of the community.

Key Concept

Early success can die out if it's not built into the fabric of community life. Sustainability comes from creating multiple mutually reinforcing cycles that support each other.

Guiding Questions

  • What new opportunities does initial success create?
  • How can benefits be shared broadly to build ongoing support?
  • What new activities or enterprises could build on this foundation?
  • How can this become "just the way things are done" rather than a special project?
[Visual Diagram: Mutually Reinforcing Success Cycles — For Placement Only]

Process

  • Revisit this 6-12 months after initial interventions
  • Map the new positive cycles that are emerging
  • Identify opportunities to strengthen and multiply them
  • Look for ways to institutionalize success (community rules, new traditions, ongoing activities)

Ingredient: Success Breeds Success

This is where the "success breeds success" dynamic becomes crucial. Initial wins create opportunities, resources, and motivation for further positive change.

Making Change Permanent

The goal is to move from a special project that requires constant attention to a new normal that maintains itself through multiple reinforcing mechanisms. This might include:

  • Economic enterprises that depend on and support restoration
  • Educational programs that build pride and understanding
  • New community traditions or celebrations
  • Recognition and support from outside organizations
  • Clear rules and enforcement mechanisms that have community buy-in

Congratulations!

You've completed the Systems Thinking tool. Remember that this is an iterative process—as you take action and learn, you'll refine your understanding and adjust your approach. The cycle of recognizing, mapping, reversing, and locking in continues as your community's situation evolves.

Explore the Ingredients for Success

Example: Apo Island

Initial success (fish recovery) led to multiple mutually reinforcing cycles:

Economic Cycle

Marine tourism income
→ Funds for community
→ More resources for protection
→ Better enforcement
→ Healthier reefs
→ More tourism

Educational Cycle

Educational programs
→ Pride in achievement
→ Stronger community identity
→ More support for protection
→ Better outcomes
→ More to teach about

Recognition Cycle

Scientific recognition
→ Outside support
→ Enhanced credibility
→ More resources
→ Better monitoring
→ More recognition

The Result: These multiple reinforcing cycles made the marine sanctuary permanent, not just a temporary project. It became integrated into the community's identity and economy.
