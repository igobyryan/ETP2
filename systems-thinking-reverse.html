Part 3

Reverse It

Identify leverage points and design interventions that can reverse vicious cycles into virtuous ones.

Breaking the Cycle

You understand the vicious cycles driving decline. This step is about identifying where you can intervene to reverse them — finding actions that can break the negative feedback loops and start positive ones.

Step 1: Identify Potential Leverage Points

Look at your vicious cycle diagram and ask:

Where could you intervene?

  • Which connections between problems could be broken?
  • Which elements could be replaced with positive alternatives?
  • Where is the community ready and able to act?
  • Are there examples from other communities that succeeded?

What resources or support do you have access to?

  • Community skills and knowledge
  • Natural resources that could regenerate
  • Traditional practices that worked in the past
  • Outside support (NGOs, government programs, etc.)
  • Economic opportunities tied to restoration
On Apo Island

Step 1: Leverage Point

The community identified that protecting a portion of their reef could allow fish populations to recover, which would then spill over into fishing areas.

Ingredient: Community Resolve

The evaluation process reveals whether you have the resolve needed. If community support seems low for a crucial intervention, that's valuable information; you may need to build resolve first through education and dialogue.

02

Step 2: Envision the Positive Flip

For each potential intervention point, imagine what success would look like:

  • If this intervention works, what positive changes would ripple through the system?
  • Could it start new positive feedback loops?
  • How would the boxes and arrows in your diagram change?
On Apo Island

Step 2: The Vision

They envisioned creating a marine sanctuary on 10% of their fishing grounds.

Fish stocks recover → More fish spill over to fishing areas → Catches improve → Community sees benefits → More support for protection → Stronger enforcement → ↺ Better recovery

[virtuous cycle]

03

Step 3: Evaluate Feasibility and Impact

The positive tipping point lever consists of the actions taken to reverse those elements plus the environmental technology used for those actions and the community organization to make the technologies and actions effective.

For each potential intervention, work through these questions as a group:

Impact Questions

  • How big a change could this make?
  • Does it address a root cause or just a symptom?
  • Will social and environmental gains go hand in hand?
  • Could it trigger positive reinforcement?
  • Is it strong enough to reverse the bottom line?

Feasibility Questions

  • What resources would this require? (money, time, skills, materials)
  • Do we have community support for this?
  • What obstacles might we face? (social, political, economic)
  • Do we have (or can we get) what we need to try this?

Risk Questions

  • What could go wrong?
  • Are there unintended consequences we should consider?
  • Is this reversible if it doesn't work?
  • Will there be pushback from other entities?
  • Will there be encroachment or takeover from other entities?

Process Tool: Simple Comparison Matrix

  • List potential interventions down the left side
  • Use columns for: Potential Impact (High/Medium/Low), Resource Needs, Community Support, Major Obstacles
  • Fill in together as a group
  • Discuss patterns and tradeoffs
On Apo Island

Step 3: Evaluation

Impact: Could reverse the fundamental problem (declining fish stocks)

Feasibility: Drew on traditional decision-making practices; representatives from all families discussed until reaching consensus

Resources: Created volunteer Marine Guard; established clear rules

04

Step 4: Choose Your Starting Point

You don't need perfect information to begin. Based on your evaluation:

  • Which 1-3 interventions seem most promising?
  • Is there a logical sequence? (some things need to happen before others)
  • Can you start with something achievable to build momentum?

Remember

  • You can try multiple interventions, especially if they reinforce each other
  • Starting with a smaller, achievable win can build the community resolve needed for bigger changes
  • You'll learn by trying. Your understanding will improve as you act
On Apo Island

Step 4: Starting Point

They began with the marine sanctuary and community management, building on their existing consensus-based decision-making traditions.
