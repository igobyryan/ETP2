Systems Thinking Tool

Finding Leverage Points: Reversing Vicious Cycles

Help communities understand what's driving ecological decline and where a single well-chosen action can reverse it.

About This Tool

Most environmental problems in rural communities aren't caused by one thing. They're the result of interconnected pressures that reinforce each other: declining fish stocks push fishers toward destructive methods, which damages reefs further, which reduces fish stocks more. These are vicious cycles, and they're why problems that seem straightforward resist simple fixes. Systems thinking gives communities a way to see those cycles clearly. Once the pattern is visible, the question shifts from "how do we fix everything?" to "where can we intervene to start reversing it?" That's a question communities can actually answer.

How This Tool Works

The four parts of this tool are structured workshops. You'll run them in sequence with your community, building understanding at each step before moving to the next. Each session produces a specific output: a list of losses and causes, a diagram of the vicious cycle, a set of evaluated interventions, a plan for sustaining what works. The community sessions that follow draw directly on this work. The systems thinking process is how you and the community build the shared picture that makes those sessions possible.

The Four-Part Process

The four parts build on each other. Don't skip ahead.

01

Recognize It

Gather the community's story of decline through small-group conversations and a community forum. You'll come out with a list of losses and causes, and a rough timeline of how things unfolded.
02

Map It

Bring the community together to turn that list into a visual diagram. You'll trace the feedback loops connecting losses and causes until the vicious cycle is visible on paper.
03

Reverse It

Look at the diagram and identify where you can realistically intervene. Evaluate options together and choose a starting point that's achievable and likely to build momentum.
04

Lock It In

As initial interventions take hold, map the new situation. Identify what's working, strengthen it, and build the economic and social connections that make recovery self-sustaining.

Who Holds the Knowledge

The knowledge is already in the community. Elders remember what the land and water looked like before the decline began. Women often hold detailed knowledge of how the ecosystem functions day to day. Young people understand current conditions and pressures. The systems thinking process draws that knowledge out and organizes it into a shared picture no single person could build alone.
