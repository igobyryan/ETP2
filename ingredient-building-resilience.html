Building Resilience

Success doesn't protect itself. Communities that build resilience into their restoration are prepared for the disruptions that will inevitably come.

Related Ingredients

→ Harmony between community and ecosystem → Social and ecological memory → Letting nature do the work

Every restoration faces disruption. Weather events, market shocks, political pressure, population change, the unexpected consequences of success itself — these aren't exceptional circumstances, they're the normal conditions in which restored ecosystems and communities have to survive. Resilience is the capacity to absorb those disruptions, reorganize around them, and keep adapting over time. It doesn't emerge automatically from success; it has to be built deliberately. Communities that build resilience alongside their restoration — strengthening governance, diversifying livelihoods, restoring ecological buffers, developing their own capacity to learn and adjust — protect what they've worked to create.

Recognize It

  • Has the community's restoration increased its ability to absorb shocks — economic, ecological, or social?

    Resilience begins with reduced vulnerability. When a community is less dependent on a single crop, a single income source, or a single governing institution, it has more capacity to absorb disruption without losing what it has built. Look for signs that the restoration itself has created buffers: healthier soils that survive drought better, diversified income that absorbs market shifts, stronger social networks that can mobilize quickly when problems arise.

  • Are the community's governance institutions strong enough to adapt when conditions change?

    Rules and institutions that worked when a restoration was new may need to evolve as the ecosystem recovers, as new pressures emerge, or as the community's composition changes. Resilient governance has the capacity to revisit, renegotiate, and revise — not just enforce what was originally decided.

  • Does the community have mechanisms for learning from experience and adjusting its approach?

    Regular reflection on what's working and what isn't — community meetings, seasonal reviews, feedback loops between ecosystem monitoring and management decisions — builds the adaptive capacity that makes resilience possible over time. The capacity to learn is as important as the capacity to absorb shocks.

  • Has the restored ecosystem developed ecological buffers that protect against disruption?

    Healthy forests reduce erosion and regulate water flow. Diverse agricultural systems buffer against weather variation and pest outbreaks. Recovered fish stocks provide a margin that allows sustainable harvesting even in difficult years. Ecological resilience and community resilience reinforce each other: the healthier the ecosystem, the more buffer it provides; the more resilient the community, the better it can protect and sustain the ecosystem.

  • Has the community anticipated the potential complications of its own success?

    Success creates new pressures: outside interest, tourism, migration, political attention, resource exploitation by people outside the community. Apo Island found that its recovered fishery attracted poachers and that tourism growth threatened the reef it had worked to protect. Anticipating these second-order effects — before they arrive — is one of the most important forms of resilience planning.

Amplify It

If resilience-building is already underway, these strategies strengthen it.

Diversify livelihoods alongside ecological restoration

Economic resilience requires that families not be entirely dependent on the restored ecosystem for their income. Home gardens, cottage industries, new enterprises, and off-farm income sources provide the financial buffer that makes it possible to manage the ecosystem sustainably rather than exploit it when times are hard. Plan for early wins in this area — visible economic gains in the first year build confidence and buy time for longer-term ecological recovery.

Strengthen governance before you need it

Governance institutions are most resilient when they're strengthened during stable periods, not stress-tested for the first time during a crisis. Regularly revisiting community rules, building clear processes for dispute resolution, and developing the next generation of local leadership all build institutional resilience that pays off when disruption arrives.

Monitor and respond — build in the feedback loops

Sustained restoration requires regular assessment of how the ecosystem is performing and how community conditions are changing. This doesn't require formal scientific monitoring; community members tracking fish catches, water levels, crop yields, or forest cover can generate the information needed to recognize when something is shifting and respond before a problem becomes a crisis.

Build relationships with outside allies before you need them

Outside organizations, government agencies, and legal authorities are most useful when the relationship already exists. Communities that have established connections to legal support, scientific expertise, or government backing can draw on those connections when threats arise — rather than trying to build them under pressure.

Plan explicitly for the complications of success

As restoration proceeds, take time to ask: what new pressures will this success attract? Who else might want access to a recovered resource? What outside interests will the community's success make visible? Building governance responses to these questions in advance is far more effective than reacting to them after they've become conflicts.

Build It

If resilience has not yet been built into the restoration, these strategies help establish it.

Start sustainability planning from the beginning, not after success

Resilience is much harder to build after the fact. The governance institutions, livelihood diversification, and ecological buffers that protect a restoration are most effectively developed as the restoration proceeds — not added on once success has been achieved. Even in early phases, it's worth asking: what would threaten what we're building, and what would protect it?

Identify the most likely disruptions and prepare for them specifically

Generic resilience is less useful than targeted preparation. What are the specific weather events, market shifts, political pressures, or social changes most likely to threaten this restoration? What would the community need to have in place to absorb each of them? Naming specific risks makes preparation concrete.

Use early wins to fund resilience-building

The economic gains from early restoration success — reduced input costs, improved harvests, new income sources — can be channeled deliberately into resilience-building activities: savings systems, community funds, infrastructure investments, or governance development. This connects short-term wins to long-term durability.

Develop community capacity for adaptive management

Adaptive management means treating the restoration as an ongoing experiment — acting on the best available knowledge, monitoring results, and adjusting based on what you learn. Building this capacity requires establishing the habits and institutions for regular reflection: community meetings with a genuine review function, willingness to revise decisions, and a culture that treats course correction as a sign of strength rather than failure.

Work Around It

When ecological resilience has been severely depleted

Some ecosystems have lost so much of their buffering capacity — soils stripped bare, watersheds degraded, fish stocks collapsed — that recovery is slow and vulnerable to setbacks. In these situations, focus first on the ecological interventions that rebuild buffer capacity most quickly: watershed protection, soil restoration, removal of the most acute pressures. Expect setbacks and plan for them rather than treating them as failures.

When governance institutions are weak or contested

Where community governance is fragile, start small — with rules that are genuinely enforceable and consequences that are actually followed through. Governance resilience is built through consistent, fair enforcement of modest commitments, not through ambitious rules that can't be maintained. Outside facilitation can help, but it can't substitute for community ownership of the governance process.

When economic pressure makes sustainable management difficult

When families are under severe financial pressure, they may have no choice but to exploit a recovered resource unsustainably. Resilience planning in these contexts has to address economic vulnerability directly — through livelihood diversification, safety net mechanisms, or targeted support during periods of stress. Ecological and economic resilience are inseparable.

See It In Action

Marine Sanctuary — Philippines

Apo Island's resilience was tested repeatedly as success brought new pressures. When tourism grew heavy enough that diving and snorkeling were damaging the coral, the community drew on the governance capacity it had built to impose restrictions — even at the cost of short-term tourism income.

When outside fishing pressure threatened the sanctuary, they drew on the legal authority they had established to defend it. The spinoffs of their success — tourist income, women's associations, strengthened community solidarity — built resilience that extended far beyond the fishery itself.

Agroforestry — Thailand

Khao Din built resilience through the diversification that agroforestry made possible. Polyculture farming reduced dependence on any single crop, soil restoration reduced dependence on outside inputs, and improved family finances reduced the desperate vulnerability that had driven overexploitation in the first place.

The revival of community social structures provided a further layer of resilience — the capacity to respond collectively to challenges as they arose. One resilience challenge remains unresolved: a trend of young people leaving for cities.

Pesticide Trap — India

In Punukula, resilience came from the self-reinforcing nature of NPM itself. When chemical pesticides stopped, natural predator populations recovered, restoring the ecological balance that had kept pest outbreaks in check.

As farm expenses and medical bills fell, families paid off debts and rebuilt financial resilience — freeing children from indentured labor, funding new community enterprises, and strengthening the mutual-aid networks that support the community through hard times. Success built confidence, and confidence built solidarity.

Rainwater Harvesting — India

The johads of Gopalpura created resilience at every level. Underground water storage provided a buffer against drought. Healthy watershed forests protected against erosion and regulated water flow.

The Gram Sabha councils — traditional village institutions revived alongside the physical restoration — provided the governance capacity to respond quickly and collectively to disruptions. The virtuous cycles that drove the restoration meant that the system became more resilient as it recovered, each gain reinforcing the next.
