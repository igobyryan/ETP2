Success Story

Gopalpura, India

How a Dried-Up Village Learned to Bank Water

In 1985, the wells in Gopalpura had been dry for years. Women and children spent up to ten hours a day walking to fetch water and firewood. Men had left for the cities. The streams were gone. So were the leopards and antelopes that had once characterized the landscape. An entire region of Rajasthan had pumped its underground water dry, and nothing in the modern toolkit seemed able to reverse it.

Then five young volunteers arrived in the village intending to start a health clinic. A village elder named Mangu Patel redirected them. What Gopalpura needed, he said, wasn't medicine. It was water.

They started digging.

A Thousand Years, Then Gone

Water has always been scarce in Alwar District. The monsoon delivers about 40 centimeters of rain across three months. For the rest of the year, the land is dry. Over millennia, farmers developed an answer: the johad, a crescent-shaped earthen dam built to intercept rainwater flowing down slopes. On the surface, a johad held water for livestock. Below the surface was its real function — holding water in place long enough for it to percolate down through soil and recharge the aquifer, sometimes as far as a kilometer away. Wells stayed full. Dry-season irrigation was possible. A johad was more than any family could build alone, so villages built and maintained them together.

This system held for centuries. It unraveled fast.

When India approached independence in the late 1940s, the local raja — fearing land seizure by the new national government — sold logging rights to commercial companies. The ancient forests came down. Without tree cover, topsoil washed off hillsides and silted up the johads. The traditional village councils that had organized communal johad maintenance — gram sabhas with participation from every family — had already been weakened by British colonial policies that eroded communal land rights and replaced shared governance with individual competition. With less incentive to protect shared resources and less institutional capacity to do so, johads fell into disrepair.

The introduction of tube well drilling equipment in the 1950s made the johads seem obsolete anyway. A dry well just needed to go deeper. This was easier than the labor of clearing johad sediment — and it worked, for a while. Vicious cycles accelerated the collapse: deeper wells sucked more water from the aquifer, dropping the water table further, which killed trees and vegetation whose roots had reached underground water, which increased erosion, which silted up the remaining johads faster. Less vegetation meant less transpiration, which meant shorter monsoons — 101 days in 1973, 55 days by 1987. Eventually the aquifer was exhausted over thousands of square kilometers. Wells went dry. Then streams. Then rivers.

One Dam, One Monsoon

Rajendra Singh had come to Gopalpura with TBS — Tarun Bharat Sangh, a young people's anti-poverty organization — to open a health clinic. Mangu Patel's redirection changed the trajectory of the entire region.

Singh and his colleagues started excavating a silted johad. Seven months of digging, nearly five meters deep. When the monsoon arrived, the pond filled. A nearby well that had been dry for years began flowing again.

The village had its answer. The following year, the whole community joined to rebuild a crumbling irrigation dam — 20 feet high, 1,400 feet long, and built on 10,000 person-days of voluntary labor. They also revived the gram sabha alongside the modern village council, because the gram sabha's total-participation, consensus-based structure was what large communal construction required. Wealthier villagers donated food to feed the workers. TBS provided technical assistance.

By 1996, Gopalpura had nine johads covering 964 hectares and holding up to 616 million liters of water. The underground water table rose from 14 meters below ground to 6.7 meters. Every well in the village was full.

The Aquifer as Bank

"It's like a bank," Rajendra Singh says. "If you make regular deposits, then you'll always have money to withdraw. If you are just taking, then you'll have no money in your bank account."

The metaphor describes exactly what happened. As water returned, the village economy rose with it. Moister subsoil meant crops needed less irrigation to thrive, and restored wells provided what irrigation was needed. Diesel fuel costs for pumping dropped 75 percent. Wheat fields expanded from 33 to 108 hectares. Farmers diversified into sugarcane, potatoes, and onions. Men came back from the cities, bringing labor for more johad construction. More johads meant more water, which sustained more trees, which protected soil from erosion, which kept the johads from silting up — the mirror image of the collapse.

Women whose days had been consumed by fetching water and firewood formed cooperatives selling dairy products, handicrafts, and soap. Girls started going to school for the first time. Villagers reforested 10 hectares at the village edge, wrote conservation rules for the new forest, and tied rakhis — kinship bracelets — around the trees as a symbol of family protection.

Spreading Across the Region

As other villages witnessed Gopalpura's transformation, they came asking for help. TBS would assist — but only after each village signed a formal contract committing the money and labor they would contribute. This requirement wasn't bureaucratic; it was the lesson Gopalpura had taught. Johads that communities hadn't built themselves didn't get maintained.

TBS publicized the johad revival through foot marches — a practice drawn from Indian spiritual tradition and Gandhi's independence movement. Thousands of people walked through the region. Thousands more visited Gopalpura. By 2005, there were 5,000 johads across 750 villages covering 8,000 square kilometers. A survey of 970 wells found all of them flowing — including 800 that had been dry just six years before. Five rivers came back to life. Forest cover in Alwar District expanded by 33 percent in 15 years.

The government of Rajasthan tried at various points to assert control — threatening TBS workers with arrest for "taking the law into their own hands," sending officials to tear down johads, selling commercial fishing rights to the reborn Arvari River. Villages resisted, sometimes by physically blocking government action. Seventy villages united to reclaim the fishing rights. Residents of the Sariska Tiger Reserve successfully sued to expel illegal marble mining operations that were draining and contaminating their underground water. The communities that had learned to build johads had also learned to defend them.

How the System Changed

Recognizing it happened through a conversation rather than a study. Mangu Patel didn't need a systems analysis to know what Gopalpura needed — the wells were dry and the women were walking ten hours a day. What TBS brought was the connection between that visible crisis and its less-visible cause: a centuries-old water management system had been dismantled, piece by piece, and no modern substitute was working. Naming that clearly was the precondition for the solution.

Mapping it revealed a cascade that traced back to deforestation. Logging drove erosion, which silted johads, which weakened the aquifer, which prompted deeper tube wells, which dropped the water table further, which killed vegetation, which increased erosion. The map also showed where the feedback could be reversed: the johad was the pivot point. Restore water retention and the whole sequence could run in the other direction.

Reversing it started with one dam in one village, timed to one monsoon. The proof was visible within months — a dry well flowing again. That immediacy mattered enormously in a community that had been told for decades that modern solutions like deeper wells were the answer. Something as ancient and simple as an earthen dam outperformed the machinery.

Locking it in required rebuilding the social infrastructure as much as the physical. The gram sabha revival wasn't incidental — it was what made sustained construction possible. Johads are communal assets that require communal maintenance. Recreating the governance structure that had built them originally was the mechanism that made the restoration permanent. And TBS's insistence that each new village contribute labor and resources before receiving help ensured that communities owned what they built. By 2005, the johad network was too large, too socially embedded, and too legally defended to be undone.

Key Ingredients

Social and ecological memory was the foundation of everything. The johad wasn't invented — it was remembered. Village elders knew how to build them and where to put them. The gram sabha tradition of consensus-based communal governance hadn't disappeared; it had been suppressed. TBS didn't introduce a foreign technology; it helped a community recover knowledge it already possessed.

Outside stimulation and facilitation arrived aimed at something else entirely. TBS came for health and education. That misdirection — or rather, that responsiveness to what the community actually needed — was itself a lesson. Rajendra Singh listened to Mangu Patel. The organization followed the village's lead, then stayed for decades providing technical assistance and connecting villages to one another.

Letting nature do the work was the elegance of the johad. The dam did nothing but hold water in place long enough for gravity and soil to do the rest. No pipes, no pumps, no infrastructure beyond an earthen wall. Nature transported water from dam to well underground, at no cost, across distances no pipe system could have served affordably.

Shared community awareness and commitment was institutionalized rather than assumed. The gram sabha revival meant that johad decisions — where to build, how to maintain, who contributed what — were made by the whole community, in meetings that ran for days if necessary, until genuine consensus emerged. That process was slow. It was also what made maintenance happen without enforcement.

Harmony between community and ecosystem deepened as the restoration progressed. The rebounding aquifer sustained trees. Trees protected soil. Protected soil kept johads clear. Clear johads recharged the aquifer. Communities that understood this feedback loop — and had built the institutions to manage it — became progressively more capable of maintaining it.

Enduring commitment of local leadership spanned decades. Rajendra Singh and TBS didn't complete the Gopalpura project and move on. They hosted the thousands of visitors who came to learn, helped new villages establish their own gram sabhas, and stood with communities when governments tried to reverse the gains. Leadership that stays the course through the long middle of restoration — after the first dramatic results but before the work is secure — is what this story turns on.

Community partner: Gopalpura Village, Alwar District, Rajasthan, India; Tarun Bharat Sangh
