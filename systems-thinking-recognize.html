Part 1

Recognize It

Gather the community's story of decline and build a shared list of losses and causes that you'll map in the next step.

Your Goal

Before you bring the full community together to map the problem, you need to understand the situation well enough to facilitate that conversation. Recognize It is fieldwork. You're building a picture of what happened, when, and to whom. The mapping session starts from shared story, not a blank page.

You'll come out of this step with two things: a rough timeline of how the decline unfolded, and a working list of losses and causes that the community will organize and diagram in Map It.

Start with Smaller Groups

Don't begin with a community-wide forum. Meet first with smaller common-interest groups: fishing families, farmers, women's groups, elders. These conversations are easier to have honestly. People speak more directly in smaller settings, and you'll hear things that won't come up in a larger group.

Aim for broad representation across these early conversations. You're not looking for consensus yet. You're listening for the range of experience.

Walk the Environment

If possible, walk the affected land or water with community members. The environment itself prompts memory. People recall specific losses when they're standing where they happened. Ask what used to be here. Ask what changed. Let them tell the story in place.

Listen First

Your role in this phase is to draw out the community's own account of what happened. Avoid offering explanations or solutions. Ask questions, then listen. The story needs to come from them.

Useful questions:

  • What did this place look like before things started to decline?
  • When did you first notice something was wrong?
  • What do you think started it?
  • What happened after that?
  • How has it affected your household? Your income? Your work?

For each problem that surfaces, follow it: what did this lead to? What else got worse because of it?

What You're Listening For

As you gather stories, keep these patterns in mind as a guide for when you have enough material to move on.

A trigger or starting point.

Was there a specific event or change that set things in motion? A new technology introduced, a policy change, a shift in land use or ownership?

A sequence of connected problems.

Does one problem seem to have caused another? You're looking for chains: this led to that, which made this worse, which caused that.

A shift from a better past.

Can elders describe conditions before the decline? That contrast sharpens the story and helps identify what the community is trying to recover.

Thresholds or turning points.

Moments when things got noticeably worse, or faster. These often mark where the system crossed into a new state.

Impacts on livelihoods.

For each environmental loss, what has it meant for people's lives, income, and work? These connections matter for building shared motivation in the mapping session.

What You're Building

As you work through these conversations, keep a running list. Two columns is enough: losses and causes. Don't worry about organizing it yet. That's Map It's job. You're collecting material.

By the time you convene the community forum, you should have a rough timeline of the decline and enough documented losses and causes that the mapping session has real content to work with from the start.

The Community Forum

Once you've completed your smaller-group fieldwork, bring the broader community together. The goal is to arrive at shared consensus on the story of decline. You're not generating the list from scratch here; you're validating, expanding, and agreeing on what you've gathered.

The forum ends when there's broad agreement on the losses, the causes, and roughly how they connect. The next step is to diagram that.

Example: Apo Island, Philippines

The Situation: Community members could see interconnected problems: declining fish catches, damaged coral reefs from dynamite and cyanide fishing, and increasing poverty.

The Memory: Elders remembered when the reefs were healthy and fish were abundant.

The Trigger: The introduction of destructive fishing methods created a vicious cycle:

Destructive fishing methods →
Reef damage →
Fewer fish →
More destructive methods →
More reef damage

The Threshold: Fishermen could no longer make a living from island fishing grounds and had to travel farther from home.

Amplifying Cycles: can reinforce existing vicious cycles and create new ones:

Fishing further away →
Less incentive to protect local waters →
More destructive methods →
More reef damage →
Even fewer fish

The Crisis: Eventually, there was nowhere close by with adequate fish, and the community faced collapse.
